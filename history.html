Through the Ages

The History of Tattoos

Ancient Origins

Tattoos are honestly one of the oldest things humans ever did to their bodies. The most famous example is Ötzi the Iceman — a mummified dude found in the Alps in 1991 who had 61 tattoos made from charcoal rubbed into cuts in his skin. He lived over 5,300 years ago which is insane to think about. Scientists think his tattoos might have been for pain relief since they were mostly on his joints.

Ancient Egypt also had tattooing, mostly on women. The tattoos found on Egyptian mummies were usually dots and lines and were connected to things like fertility and religion. So basically tattoos have always meant something deeper than just looking cool.

Polynesia & the Pacific

Some of the best tattoo traditions in the world come from the Pacific Islands. In places like Hawaii, Samoa, and New Zealand the tattoos weren't just decorative — they told you who someone was, what family they came from, and what they had done in their life. The Maori people called their facial tattoos ta moko and each one was completely unique to that person like a fingerprint.

The word "tattoo" actually comes from the Tahitian word tatau. Captain James Cook's crew heard it during their 1769 voyage to the Pacific and brought the word back to Europe along with some tattoos of their own. So Hawaii and the Pacific basically gave tattoos to the whole western world which is pretty cool especially since we're in Hawaii right now.

Japan and Irezumi

Japan has one of the most detailed tattoo traditions anywhere. Its called irezumi which means "inserting ink." Japanese tattooing goes back thousands of years but really became an art form during the Edo period when full body suits became popular among certain groups like gamblers and firefighters. The imagery is super symbolic — koi fish mean perseverance, dragons mean wisdom, and cherry blossoms represent how life is short and beautiful.

Traditional Japanese tattooing is done with a technique called tebori where the artist uses a wooden or metal rod to hand-poke the ink in one dot at a time. A lot of traditional Japanese artists still do it this way today because it gives a softer look than a machine.

Sailors and Western Tattooing

In the US and Europe tattoos spread mostly because of sailors who got them in Pacific ports and brought the practice home. Tattoo shops started opening near ports and the classic imagery — anchors, swallows, pin-ups, dice — became the foundation of what we now call American Traditional. Norman "Sailor Jerry" Collins was one of the most important artists in this era and he worked right here in Honolulu in Chinatown which I thought was really interesting to learn about.

In 1891 a guy named Samuel O'Reilly invented the first electric tattoo machine based on an invention by Thomas Edison. This was a huge deal because it made tattooing faster and more available to regular people, not just sailors or circus performers.

The 20th Century

For a long time tattoos were seen as kind of trashy or rebellious in mainstream American culture. They were associated with bikers, prisoners, and outcasts. But starting in the 70s and 80s a new wave of artists started pushing tattooing as a real art form. They called it the "tattoo renaissance" and it completely changed how people thought about getting inked.

Today about 1 in 5 Americans have a tattoo and the industry keeps growing. Styles have exploded way beyond the old sailor stuff into realism, watercolor, fine line, blackwork, and tons of others. Its definitely not just for rebels anymore.

Key Timeline

  • 3300 BCE — Ötzi the Iceman (oldest known tattoos)
  • 2000 BCE — Tattooed mummies found in Egypt
  • 1769 — Captain Cook brings "tattoo" to the English language
  • 1891 — First electric tattoo machine invented
  • 1970s-80s — Tattoo renaissance begins in the US
  • Today — Global multi-billion dollar industry
